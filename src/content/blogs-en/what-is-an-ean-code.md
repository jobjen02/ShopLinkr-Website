<p>In the world of e-commerce, retail and inventory management, the EAN code plays a key role. These unique product codes are essential for tracking and managing products throughout the chain. In this article you will learn what EAN codes are exactly, how they are structured, why they matter so much, how to request them and why you cannot make them up yourself.</p>
<h2>What is an EAN code?</h2>
<p>EAN stands for European Article Number, known internationally as the International Article Number since 2009. The system was developed to track products unambiguously throughout a supply chain. Every EAN code is unique and consists of a series of 13 digits. You recognize it as the barcode on the packaging, the one you scan at the checkout or in your warehouse.</p>
<h2>The structure of an EAN code</h2>
<p>The 13 digits of an EAN code are not random, they follow a fixed structure:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Country code</strong>: the first 2 to 3 digits indicate the country where the manufacturer is registered.</li>
<li><strong>Manufacturer code</strong>: the next digits (usually 4 to 5) identify the specific company.</li>
<li><strong>Product code</strong>: the manufacturer assigns these digits itself to each individual product.</li>
<li><strong>Check digit</strong>: the final digit is a control number that calculates whether the whole code is correct.</li>
</ol>
<h2>Why EAN codes matter so much</h2>
<p>EAN codes are more than stripes on a package, they are an essential link in modern trade and logistics. Below you can see everything they do.</p>
<h3>Global standardization</h3>
<p>EAN codes provide a uniform way to identify products worldwide. This allows a product to be recognized and traded across companies, countries and languages.</p>
<h3>The backbone of inventory management</h3>
<p>EAN codes are the foundation of virtually every inventory management system. They make it possible to track stock accurately and to run reliable reports.</p>
<h3>Faster sales transactions</h3>
<p>At the checkout, EAN codes enable fast and accurate processing. That reduces the chance of errors and creates a smoother customer experience.</p>
<h3>Essential for online selling</h3>
<p>Online marketplaces often require an EAN code before you are allowed to offer a product. That way consumers know exactly what they are buying and products can be compared properly.</p>
<h3>Traceability and safety</h3>
<p>EAN codes make products traceable throughout the entire chain, from factory to end user. That matters for logistics, but also for consumer safety in the event of recalls, for example.</p>
<h3>Support for automation</h3>
<p>In an era where automation plays a leading role, EAN codes help automate inbound and outbound flows of goods in warehouses.</p>
<h3>Insight into sales and analytics</h3>
<p>Companies use EAN codes to collect sales data and analyze patterns. That leads to better decisions in product range and marketing.</p>
<h3>Indispensable for omnichannel</h3>
<p>For companies that serve multiple channels, EAN codes are essential for keeping product data consistent.</p>
<h3>Legal compliance</h3>
<p>In many countries, EAN codes are mandatory for certain product categories. Using official codes is therefore also part of running a business responsibly.</p>
<h3>Reliability and consumer trust</h3>
<p>By using official EAN codes you show that you are part of a worldwide system of trustworthy trade. That strengthens the trust of your customers.</p>
<h2>How do you get an EAN code?</h2>
<p>Do you want to request EAN codes for your products? Then you go through these steps:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Register with GS1</strong>: GS1 is the international non-profit that issues EAN codes. Register your company with the Dutch branch in order to receive codes.</li>
<li><strong>Request codes</strong>: after registering, you request the number of codes that suits your product range.</li>
<li><strong>Codes are assigned</strong>: you receive a unique manufacturer code, which you use to compose your own EAN code per product.</li>
<li><strong>Account for the costs</strong>: registration and codes come with costs, depending on the country and the number.</li>
</ol>
<h2>When do you need an EAN code?</h2>
<p>Whether you need an EAN code depends on a few factors:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Type of product and sales channel</strong>: for most physical products in shops or on large marketplaces such as Amazon and eBay, EAN codes are mandatory.</li>
<li><strong>International selling</strong>: are you selling across the border? Then an EAN code is virtually indispensable for logistics and customs.</li>
<li><strong>Inventory management</strong>: EAN codes are also very handy for your own inventory management and track &amp; trace.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Why you cannot make up EAN codes yourself</h2>
<p>EAN codes are not just random numbers. They have a specific function in global trade and inventory management. That is why you have to request them officially.</p>
<h3>Global uniqueness</h3>
<p>Every product must have a unique identification. If you make up a code yourself, you run the risk that it already exists, with errors throughout the entire chain as a result.</p>
<h3>International compatibility</h3>
<p>EAN codes have to be recognized by all parties in the chain, from manufacturer to retailer. Self-invented codes are not recognized by international systems.</p>
<h3>Data integrity</h3>
<p>Reliable inventory data is the basis for good reporting and business planning. Unreliable codes lead to discrepancies and financial losses.</p>
<h3>Retailer requirements</h3>
<p>Many retailers, such as Amazon, only accept products with an official EAN code. Self-invented codes are rejected.</p>
<h3>Legal and commercial obligations</h3>
<p>Certain product categories are subject to legal labeling requirements. Failing to comply with these can lead to fines.</p>
<h3>Protection against fraud</h3>
<p>Making up EAN codes yourself can be seen as an attempt at fraud, especially if it leads to the sale of poorly described or untraceable products.</p>
<h3>Fast recalls</h3>
<p>In the event of a product recall, the right products need to be found quickly. That only works with official EAN codes.</p>
<h3>Marketing and sales analytics</h3>
<p>EAN codes make it possible to analyze sales patterns and develop targeted marketing. Without valid codes, those analyses become unreliable.</p>
<p>In short, making up EAN codes yourself undermines the whole purpose of a standardized system. Request them officially from GS1 and your products will be recognizable, traceable and sellable worldwide.</p>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>EAN codes are a foundation underpinning modern trade and e-commerce. They provide a universal standard for product identification, are crucial for inventory management and keep your logistics processes running smoothly. Official codes through GS1 are essential for recognizability and reliability. For any webshop or retailer that wants to grow along with an increasingly connected world, EAN codes are indispensable.</p>
