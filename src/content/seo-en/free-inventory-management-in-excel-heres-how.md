<p>Free inventory management in Excel is the logical first step for many new online stores: you already have the program, and you can be up and running within an hour. In this article you'll build a working sheet yourself, learn the formulas you actually need, and see where Excel runs out of road once you start selling across multiple channels.</p>

<h2>Building your own inventory sheet</h2>
<p>You don't need to download a ready-made template. A handful of columns already gives you a usable overview that you can expand later.</p>
<ol>
<li>Create columns for <strong>SKU</strong>, product name, location, quantity in stock, cost price, and selling price.</li>
<li>Add a <strong>minimum stock</strong> column: the level at which you want to reorder.</li>
<li>Turn your data into an Excel table (Insert, Table). New rows then automatically inherit your formulas and formatting.</li>
<li>Update the quantity after every sale, purchase, or return, so the sheet keeps matching your physical stock.</li>
</ol>
<p>Use separate tabs per product category or warehouse location as your catalog grows. That keeps things organized and saves you from scrolling through hundreds of rows.</p>

<h2>Formulas that make inventory management smarter</h2>
<p>The real power of Excel comes from a few simple formulas that handle the math for you:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Stock value</strong>: multiply quantity by cost price and add it up with <strong>SUM</strong> to get the total value of your inventory.</li>
<li><strong>Reorder alert</strong>: with an <strong>IF</strong> formula, "reorder" appears automatically the moment a quantity drops below your minimum stock.</li>
<li><strong>Conditional formatting</strong>: color rows red where stock is too low, so you can see at a glance what needs attention.</li>
<li><strong>Pivot tables</strong>: summarize your sales by product or by month to spot trends and seasonal peaks.</li>
</ul>
<p>Also set up data validation on the columns where it makes sense. That prevents typos and keeps your numbers reliable.</p>
