<p>With a backorder, you accept an order for a product that is not in stock at that moment. Instead of losing the sale, you place the order on hold and fulfill it as soon as new stock arrives. For webshops and marketplace sellers, a backorder is a way to capture demand without having to keep everything in stock all the time. In this article you'll learn what a backorder is, how the process works, and how to manage backorders smoothly.</p>

<h2>How a backorder works</h2>
<p>A backorder happens when a customer orders something that is temporarily sold out. The order is accepted and flagged, so it's clear which products and how many of them still need to be delivered. Once new stock comes in, you pick the order back up and ship it.</p>
<ol>
<li><p>The customer places an order for a product that is not in stock.</p></li>
<li><p>The order is accepted and recorded as a backorder, with an expected delivery time for the customer.</p></li>
<li><p>You reorder the product from your supplier or manufacturer.</p></li>
<li><p>As soon as the stock arrives, the order is processed, packed, and shipped.</p></li>
</ol>
<p>Your <a href="/en/features/inventory">inventory management</a> is critical here: you need to know exactly what's on the shelf, what's on the way, and what's already promised to customers. Otherwise you'll commit to products you can't deliver.</p>

<h2>Why backorders are useful</h2>
<p>Well-managed backorders keep customers who would otherwise turn to a competitor. You capture demand without holding large buffer stocks, which cuts storage costs and the risk of dead stock. Backorders also give you valuable signals: a product that is constantly on backorder is a clear sign to adjust your <a href="/en/features/purchase-advice">purchasing</a> and stock forecast.</p>
<p>The flip side is that a backorder only pays off when the communication is right. An unexpected wait or a vague status update costs trust. Keep delivery times realistic and proactively let customers know about delays.</p>

<h2>Frequently asked questions about backorders</h2>
<h3>What happens if a backorder can't be fulfilled?</h3>
<p>You let the customer know. They can then choose to wait longer or cancel the order. Clear communication prevents most of the frustration here.</p>
<h3>Can you cancel a backorder?</h3>
<p>Yes. As long as the order hasn't been processed and shipped, a backorder can be canceled.</p>
<h3>Is a backorder the same as a delayed delivery?</h3>
<p>No. A backorder means the product is temporarily out of stock but is intentionally being restocked and shipped later. It is not a sign of poor service, as long as you hit the delivery time you promised.</p>
